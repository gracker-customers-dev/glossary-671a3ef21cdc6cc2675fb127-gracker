digital rights management

A set of technologies and policies used to control access to and usage of digital content.

Description

Digital Rights Management (DRM) refers to a collection of access control technologies that are used to protect copyrighted digital content from unauthorized use and distribution. It encompasses a variety of techniques that manage how digital media, such as music, movies, e-books, and software, can be accessed, copied, and shared. DRM systems can impose restrictions on the number of devices on which a piece of content can be used, limit the duration of access, and even track how and when the content is accessed. The overarching goal of DRM is to safeguard the rights of content creators and distributors while also balancing consumer access to digital products. However, DRM has been a subject of controversy due to its potential to restrict legitimate use and its implications for user privacy and ownership. As digital content consumption continues to grow, the evolution of DRM technologies is crucial to addressing the challenges posed by piracy and unauthorized distribution.

Examples

  • Apple's FairPlay DRM for music and video content.
  • Adobe's DRM for e-books through Adobe Digital Editions.

Additional Information

  • DRM can be implemented through encryption, license agreements, and server-side controls.
  • Critics argue that DRM can infringe on users' rights and limit fair use.
